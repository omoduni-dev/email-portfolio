Capital Market Overview

The Nigerian bourse continued to record positive market sentiment, with the NGX All Share Index and total market capitalization rising week on week basis by 3.96% and 3.94% to close at 225,724.33 points and ₦145.34trillion respectively. Momentum accelerated in the week was adduced to investor confidence strengthened by favorable corporate earnings released, dividend announcements, bank recapitalization mandates, regulatory reforms, robust domestic investor participation, and increased technological adoption, FTSE Russell’s reclassification of Nigeria to "Frontier Market" status, signaling increased global liquidity, market resilience and growth in both equity and fixed-income sectors. As reflected in month to date and quarter to date gains of 12.14%, alongside a robust year to date return of 45.05%.

Market breadth remained positive at 1.62x, with 42 gainers led by ACADEMY, UPDC, HMCALL, ZICHIS, and WEMABANK outpacing 26decliners. The top losers included MEYER, TRANSEXPR, CILEASING, OMATEK, and ETRANZACT. Sectoral performance was broadly positive, as gains were recorded in the Banking (+1.86%), Insurance (+0.06%), Consumer Goods (+0.12%), Oil & Gas (+0.24%), and Industrial Goods (+4.36%) sectors, while the Commodity sector closed flat. However, Trading activity was mixed, as turnover and deal count climbed 16.76% and 4.09% to ₦44.51 billion and 55,232 transactions respectively, while volume edged down 6.04% to 627.62 million shares.

Forty-six (46) equities appreciated in price during the week, lower than sixty-one (61) equities in the previous week. Fifty-three (53) equities depreciated in price, higher than thirty-six (36) equities in the previous week, while forty-seven (47) equities remained unchanged, lower than forty-nine (49) recorded in the previous week.

Foreign Reserve & Exchange Market

Gross official Foreign reserves posted a decline of US$172.53 million during the week to close at $48.44 billion (as at 23 April, 2026), compared with $48.62 billion recorded the previous week while the naira weakened across both market segments, depreciated by 0.33% to ₦1,358.44/$ at the NAFEM window and declining a further 0.22% to ₦1,369/$ in the parallel market, reflecting broad-based selling pressure on the local currency across both the official and informal foreign exchange segment.
